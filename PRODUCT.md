# Product

## Register

product

## Users

People living with PTSD who own a dog. Hebrew-speaking, likely Israeli market. They use the app throughout the day — morning routine, afternoon break, evening wind-down — often during states of anxiety, dissociation, or cognitive fog. The app must work when the user is not at their best.

## Product Purpose

Petter is a daily dog care companion for people with PTSD. It helps owners maintain consistent care routines by framing tasks as bonding rituals with their dog — not obligations to be tracked. Completing a task is a reason to celebrate. Missing one is never punished. Success looks like an owner who feels connected to their dog and capable of caring for them, even on hard days.

## Brand Personality

Calm, Tender, Playful. The app is a gentle, steady presence — like a good friend who celebrates small wins without making a fuss about bad days. Warm without being saccharine. Joyful without being loud.

## Anti-references

- **Clinical / medical apps**: Cold whites, sterile form-heavy layouts, anything that feels like a health dashboard or symptom tracker.
- **Mindfulness / meditation apps**: Foggy gradients, ethereal type, over-designed serenity (Calm, Headspace aesthetic). Too passive, too hushed.
- **Baby / children's apps**: Primary colors, cartoonish rounded illustrations, excessive cuteness. The users are adults going through something real.
- **Gamified habit trackers**: Duolingo-style streaks, XP bars, red failure indicators, competitive language. The app must never make the user feel bad for a missed day.

## Design Principles

1. **Never make the user feel bad.** No streaks, no overdue states, no red indicators. Each day starts clean. The past is accessible but never weaponized.
2. **Celebrate care, not performance.** A completed task is a bonding moment, not a checkbox. The visual language must reflect that — celebration is built into the core interaction.
3. **Zero friction to start.** Two screens to a working routine. The app should be useful before the user has customized anything.
4. **Softness in everything.** Tone, color, motion, copy — all of it should feel like a gentle hand, not a system demanding compliance.
5. **Rituals, not tasks.** The framing is always about the bond between owner and dog. Copy, naming, and flow reinforce this consistently.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- Hebrew (RTL) is a first-class requirement, not an afterthought. All layouts must mirror correctly.
- WCAG AA minimum. High contrast for body text — PTSD populations may use the app during high-stress moments when visual clarity matters most.
- Reduced motion support required. Hypervigilance makes sudden or unexpected animation harmful. All animations must respect `prefers-reduced-motion`.
- Cognitive load must be minimal. One clear action per screen. Never more than one primary call to action visible at once.
